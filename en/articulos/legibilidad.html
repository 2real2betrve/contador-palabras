Flesch-Kincaid Formula: Write to be Understood

Published on April 29, 2026 • Reading time: 4 min

In the world of digital writing, there is a persistent myth: using big words and long, complex sentences makes you sound smarter and more professional. Reality (and analytics data) tells us exactly the opposite. If a user enters your blog and feels like they are reading a technical engineering manual, the bounce rate skyrockets. This is where the famous Flesch-Kincaid metric comes in.

What is the Flesch Readability Formula?

Originally developed in 1948 by Austrian-American author Rudolf Flesch, this mathematical formula was designed to measure how difficult a text is to understand in English. Later, J. Peter Kincaid adapted it for the United States Navy. Today, it is the global standard in marketing, SEO, and UX design.

The formula evaluates two main variables:

  • Average sentence length: (Number of words divided by the number of sentences).
  • Average word length: (Number of syllables divided by the number of words).

The Scoring Scale

In WordCount Pro, we utilize the standard Flesch Reading Ease formula. The scoring scale ranges from 0 to 100:

  • 90-100 (Very Easy): Easily understood by an 11-year-old student. Ideal for comics and basic texts.
  • 60-80 (Easy to Normal): Understood by 13 to 15-year-old teenagers. This is your target for SEO content!
  • 30-50 (Difficult): College level. Dense and requires concentration.
  • 0-30 (Very Difficult): Complex academic, scientific, or legal texts.

Why it Matters for SEO and Sales

Search engine algorithms pay close attention to Dwell Time. If you write with a Flesch-Kincaid score of 20 (very difficult), casual readers will click the "Back" button almost immediately. This sends a negative signal to Google, indicating that your page did not resolve the search intent in an accessible way.

3 Quick Tips to Improve Your Score:

  1. Shorten your sentences: If a sentence has more than two commas, you should probably split it into two sentences separated by a period.
  2. Use short paragraphs: On the internet, a paragraph shouldn't have more than 3-4 lines. White space is visually crucial.
  3. Choose the simple word: Instead of "utilize", say "use". Instead of "subsequently", say "after".

Conclusion

Your goal as a writer or copywriter is not to win a Nobel Prize in literature, but to communicate an idea as quickly and frictionlessly as possible. Paste your next draft into our counter and check that your readability badge is green. Your readers (and your conversions) will thank you.
